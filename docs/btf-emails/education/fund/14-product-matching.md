**Subject (Fallback):** LOC, term loan, equipment financing -- which one fits

```
Hey {{first_name}},

You can have a great profile in the {{current_phase}} phase and still lose money by picking the wrong product for the job. Capital is not interchangeable. Each product has a use case where it shines and a use case where it bleeds you dry.

Here is the matchup.

LINE OF CREDIT (LOC)
Best for: variable working capital, payroll bridging, inventory cycles, opportunistic buys
Why: you only pay interest on what you draw. Draw $20K, pay it back in 45 days, only pay 45 days of interest. Redraw when needed.
Worst for: long-term assets you will hold for years. The variable rate kills you over time.
Typical terms: 10-25% APR, $25K-$500K limits, revolving for 1-3 years before renewal review.

TERM LOAN
Best for: large one-time investments with predictable ROI (acquisition, build-out, single piece of equipment over $50K)
Why: fixed rate, fixed payment, fixed term. You know exactly what it costs over 3-7 years.
Worst for: working capital. You take $100K, the cash sits, and you pay interest on idle money.
Typical terms: 7-15% APR (SBA: 10-13%, conventional: 8-12%), 3-10 year terms.

EQUIPMENT FINANCING
Best for: trucks, machinery, computers, dental chairs -- anything with a serial number that holds value
Why: the equipment is the collateral, so the rate is lower than unsecured. Often 100% financing available.
Worst for: anything that depreciates fast or has no resale value. Software, marketing, inventory.
Typical terms: 6-12% APR, 3-7 year terms based on equipment life.

INVOICE FACTORING
Best for: B2B businesses with slow-paying clients (net-60 or net-90 enterprise customers)
Why: turn a 60-day receivable into cash today for a 2-4% fee. Stops cash crunches without taking debt.
Worst for: B2C businesses or anyone with short payment cycles already.
Typical terms: 2-5% fee per invoice, no APR (technically not a loan).

MERCHANT CASH ADVANCE (MCA)
Best for: see previous email -- almost nothing
Why: covered last email
Use case: only as a last-resort bridge to a specific, guaranteed receivable

THE RULE
Match the term length of the funding to the useful life of what you are buying. Working capital = revolving credit. Equipment with 5-year life = 5-year financing. Long-term asset = long-term debt. Mismatch the term and you pay double or starve cash flow.
```
